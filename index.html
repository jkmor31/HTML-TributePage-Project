Frederick Sanger

Frederick Sanger

Timeline of His Life

  • 1918 - Born in Rendcomb, a small village in Gloucestershire, England.
  • 1939 - Attended college at University of Cambridge, studying natural sciences
  • 1943 - Graduated PhD program from Cambridge and moved to London to work for the National Institute for Medical Research.
  • 1951-1952 - Discovered the complete amino acid sequence for bovine insulin A and B polypeptide chains.
  • 1958 - Won the Nobel prize in Chemistry for determining that every protein had a unique sequence.
  • 1964 - Discovered the formylmethionine tRNA which initiates protein synthesis in bacteria.
  • 1977 - Created the "Sanger Method", which introduced the "dideoxy" chain-termination method for sequencing DNA molecules.
  • 1980 - Won his second Nobel prize in Chemistry for his chain-termination method, which was later used in the sequencing of the human genome.
  • 1993 - Helped open the Sanger Institue a few miles from his home. Now it is one of the world's largest research centers in human genome sequencing.
  • 2013 - Sanger died in Cambridge.
